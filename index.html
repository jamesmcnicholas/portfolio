$Audiosplit

Keystrokes with your voice

I ran out of fingers - so I decided i'd use my voice... This project starts with an obscure game from my childhood called Globy. Once a year or so I find myself scouring the wayback machine for the download link so I can replay it. This time however, I was thinking about how long it took me to beat as a kid with my dad - it was evening entertainment for weeks. But now that I know my way around a mouse and keyboard, the game is a bit easier - but how much easier exactly? Could i beat it in one sitting? I did. Then i found someone speedrunning the game - 1 hour and 42 minutes. I could beat that. Several days later and my housemate and I had the time down to 34 minutes. But speedrunning involves multitasking, hitting buttons to mark "splits" when you finish a section. Whilst i'm better than 12 year old me at games, my multitasking skills are still lacking. So I decided to employ voice recognition to help out. This could of course all be run locally, but my dusty old pi B+ was giving me the puppy dog eyes

>$Poor Man's Spotify

I'm pretty frugal...

Paying for subscription services has always tickled my wallet the wrong way, but exploring music is pretty important as a teenager. Long before Google shut down Google Play Music, you could upload your own MP3s and have their service sync them out to your devices. This gave me an idea - use youtube-dl to grab music from youtube, scrape its metadata for cover art and details, then upload to GPM. Using Gitub's student pack, I fired up a vm on Digital Ocean's cloud platform and wrote a quick landing page to grab a youtube URL. Then, I did some hunting and found the unnoficial Google Music API. One quick python script later and it was good to go - any song I wanted on all my devices. Eventually however, I did begrudgingly make the switch to spotify.

>$Hours of Tunes

Visualising copyrighted music for views

Okay, so this one's not the most ethical of my projects - but in my defence, I was 16. Not really a software project, but following in the footsteps of a friend who uploaded a 10 hour long version of Hozier's Take Me To Church to youtube. Seeing its success (Over a million views), I thought I could whack some after effects visualisers on some music and rake in some internet fame. To my shock, it worked... to an extent. The views began to rack up, but the subscribers dwindled - as did the revenue due to the copyrighted music. Still, its cool to lay claim to a channel that's accumulated over 800 thousand views, and over 21 years of total watch time. I think the revenue speaks for itself! /s Don't worry - all the videos were swiftly copyright claimed so the revenue went to whichever megalabel owns the rights.
